## Intelligence & Cognition (12 questions)
--------------------------------------------------

1. Declaring the mind and body to be entirely separate substances is the philosophical position known as…
   1) [CORRECT] mind-body dualism
      → Right — mind and body treated as distinct substances.
   2) [WRONG] empiricism
      → That's the view that all knowledge comes from sensory experience.
   3) [WRONG] nativism
      → That's the view that some knowledge is innate, not learned.
   4) [WRONG] connectionism
      → That's a modern cognitive-architecture view, not a mind-body position.

2. The view that our thoughts are created solely through stimulation of the senses is…
   1) [CORRECT] empiricism
      → Right — knowledge built entirely from sensory input.
   2) [WRONG] nativism
      → That holds we're born with some innate knowledge.
   3) [WRONG] mind-body dualism
      → That's about mind and body being separate substances, not where knowledge comes from.
   4) [WRONG] behaviourism
      → That's about stimulus-response learning, not the origin of knowledge per se.

3. The view that humans are born with innate knowledge enabling language and conscious experience is…
   1) [CORRECT] nativism
      → Right — some knowledge or capacity is innate.
   2) [WRONG] empiricism
      → That holds all knowledge comes from sensory experience.
   3) [WRONG] connectionism
      → That's a cognitive-architecture model, not a claim about innate knowledge.
   4) [WRONG] symbolic processing
      → That's a model of mental computation, not a nativist claim.

4. Early brain-injury case studies (a patient who could only say one syllable, and another with comprehension deficits) were used to show that…
   1) [CORRECT] different brain regions are specialised for different functions, yet highly interconnected

   2) [WRONG] the brain processes all information identically everywhere

   3) [WRONG] language has no biological basis

   4) [WRONG] intelligence cannot be measured scientifically

## Learning & Memory (12 questions)
--------------------------------------------------

13. Long-term memory is generally split into explicit (what happened, when) and…
   1) [CORRECT] implicit memory (how to do something)
      → Right — explicit vs implicit (declarative vs procedural).
   2) [WRONG] short-term memory
      → That's a separate stage (storage duration), not a type of long-term memory.
   3) [WRONG] working memory
      → That's the active-manipulation system, not a long-term memory type.
   4) [WRONG] sensory memory
      → That's a very brief initial registration of input, not long-term storage.

14. A strong, distinctive cue is more likely than a weak cue to…
   1) [CORRECT] reliably elicit the same retrieval response most of the time
      → Right — strong cues trigger a specific reaction reliably.
   2) [WRONG] have no effect on retrieval at all
      → Cues by definition help guide retrieval, weak or strong.
   3) [WRONG] erase the original memory entirely
      → Cues support retrieval; they don't erase memories.
   4) [WRONG] work only in short-term memory
      → Cue strength affects retrieval broadly, not only short-term recall.

15. In learning, initial acquisition is typically fastest with constant reinforcement, but behaviour is most stable with…
   1) [CORRECT] intermittent, sometimes-absent reinforcement
      → Right — uncertainty about reward makes the behaviour 'stick'.
   2) [WRONG] reinforcement on every single occurrence
      → That speeds initial learning but doesn't make it most durable.
   3) [WRONG] no reinforcement at all
      → Without any reinforcement, behaviour tends to extinguish.
   4) [WRONG] punishment only, never reward
      → Punishment reduces behaviour; this is about what stabilises it, not removes it.

16. University study advice based on memory research includes working in focused blocks (e.g., 25 minutes followed by a mandatory break), which reflects the principle that…
   1) [CORRECT] working memory has limited capacity, so deep, undistracted work beats constant shallow multitasking
      → Right — capacity limits motivate structured, distraction-free study blocks.
   2) [WRONG] memory capacity is effectively unlimited if you just try harder
      → The advice exists precisely because capacity is limited.
   3) [WRONG] repetition alone, with no variation, is the most effective study method
      → Plain repetition without variety is described as unattractive to the brain.
   4) [WRONG] short-term memory and long-term memory are identical
      → The distinction between the two is part of why structured study habits matter.

17. A widely cited line about human progress argues that what makes our species capable, despite not being individually much smarter than other animals, is our capacity to…
   1) [CORRECT] transmit accumulated knowledge across generations
      → Right — cultural transmission of knowledge, not individual brilliance.
   2) [WRONG] forget unimportant details quickly
      → Forgetting isn't the cited advantage.
   3) [WRONG] physically outcompete other species
      → Physical competition isn't the point being made.
   4) [WRONG] avoid all forms of cognitive offloading
      → The point is about transmitting knowledge, not avoiding offloading.

18. Word-embedding techniques such as Word2Vec rely on which learning principle to associate words?

      → Right — words appearing close together become associated.
   2) [WRONG] competition among predictors
      → That's about blocking redundant cues, not co-occurrence-based association.
   3) [WRONG] configural learning
      → That's about binding multiple features into one bundle, not word co-occurrence.
   4) [WRONG] unlearning
      → That's about a pairing fading over time, not forming new associations.

19. Captchas that show increasingly blurred images are used to train models on which learning principle?

   2) [WRONG] temporal contiguity

   3) [WRONG] extinction
      → That's about a learned pairing fading, unrelated to Captcha training.
   4) [WRONG] competition among predictors
      → That's about blocking redundant cues, not generalising across blurred images.

   2) [WRONG] become measurably worse at using search engines over time

   3) [WRONG] accurately judge their own memory abilities

   4) [WRONG] permanently lose previously stored long-term memories

   3) [WRONG] identical essay quality regardless of condition

22. Sharing tasks like remembering gift lists with others, rather than memorising everything yourself, is an everyday example of…

   2) [WRONG] configural learning

   4) [WRONG] extinction

## Intelligence & Cognition  (11 questions)
--------------------------------------------------

1. A pigeon gets food whenever it happens to peck a lever, and pecking then increases. This is…
   1) [CORRECT] operant conditioning
        -> Right — a consequence (food) shapes the behaviour.
   2) [WRONG]   classical conditioning
        -> That's pairing two stimuli (tone→food), not learning from a consequence.
   3) [WRONG]   configural learning
        -> That's learning several features together as one bundle.
   4) [WRONG]   temporal contiguity
        -> That's association formed when two things are active close together in time.

2. A tone repeatedly sounds just before food, until the tone alone triggers salivation. This is…
   1) [CORRECT] classical conditioning
        -> Right — a neutral stimulus paired with food comes to trigger the response.
   2) [WRONG]   operant conditioning
        -> That's learning driven by the consequences of a behaviour, not stimulus pairing.
   3) [WRONG]   extinction
        -> That's a learned link fading when the consequence stops.
   4) [WRONG]   generalization
        -> That's transferring a response to similar new situations.

3. Describing the mind as distributed units spread across layers, rather than a manipulator of explicit symbols, is the…
   1) [CORRECT] connectionist model
        -> Right — mind as distributed units across layers.
   2) [WRONG]   symbolic model
        -> That's the 'mind as computer' view: explicit symbols manipulated by rules.
   3) [WRONG]   behaviourist model
        -> That explains behaviour by stimulus–response learning, ignoring inner representations.
   4) [WRONG]   nativist model
        -> That's the view that key knowledge is innate rather than learned.

4. Doing well on one cognitive test predicts doing well on others. This shared factor is…
   1) [CORRECT] g (general intelligence)
        -> Right — the shared variance across cognitive tasks.
   2) [WRONG]   fluid intelligence
        -> That's reasoning speed on novel problems — a sub-factor, not the general one.
   3) [WRONG]   crystallized intelligence
        -> That's accumulated knowledge — a sub-factor, not the general one.
   4) [WRONG]   working memory
        -> That's short-term holding/manipulation of information, not the general factor.

5. Accumulated vocabulary, facts and problem-solving experience make up which kind of intelligence?
   1) [CORRECT] crystallized
        -> Right — accumulated knowledge and experience.
   2) [WRONG]   fluid
        -> That's raw processing speed on new problems, which declines with age.
   3) [WRONG]   general (g)
        -> That's the overall factor across tasks, not the knowledge-specific one.
   4) [WRONG]   emotional
        -> 'Emotional intelligence' isn't the knowledge/experience factor described.

6. The raw speed of information processing, which tends to decline with age, is…
   1) [CORRECT] fluid intelligence
        -> Right — processing speed, which declines with age.
   2) [WRONG]   crystallized intelligence
        -> That's knowledge/experience, which holds up better over time.
   3) [WRONG]   the g factor
        -> That's the general factor across tasks, not the speed-specific one.
   4) [WRONG]   self-efficacy
        -> That's a belief about your own capability, not a type of intelligence.

7. Carefully working line-by-line through a tax form draws mainly on…
   1) [CORRECT] System II — slow, effortful, deliberate
        -> Right — the effortful mode for complex, step-by-step work.
   2) [WRONG]   System I — fast, automatic, intuitive
        -> That's quick, automatic processing — the opposite of effortful checking.
   3) [WRONG]   priming — spreading activation
        -> That's one cue making related ideas easier to access, not deliberate reasoning.
   4) [WRONG]   crystallized intelligence
        -> That's stored knowledge, not a mode of effortful processing.

8. In the broad cognitive-science sense, 'cognition' is best captured as…
   1) [CORRECT] all processing of input, even imagined input with no stimulus present
        -> Right — the definition is deliberately broad.
   2) [WRONG]   conscious reasoning, as opposed to automatic habit
        -> Too narrow — cognition includes automatic processes too.
   3) [WRONG]   the emotional appraisal that makes events feel significant
        -> That describes affect/emotion, not the broad processing definition.
   4) [WRONG]   the accurate storage and retrieval of past experience
        -> That's just memory — one part of cognition, not the whole.

9. The lecture contrasts humans as 'epistemic agents' with current LLMs framed as…
   1) [CORRECT] 'stochastic parrots' producing plausible continuations
        -> Right — fluent output without genuine understanding.
   2) [WRONG]   'symbolic reasoners' applying logical rules
        -> That describes old rule-based AI, not how LLMs are framed here.
   3) [WRONG]   'connectionist minds' with genuine understanding
        -> The framing is the opposite — plausible output isn't true understanding.
   4) [WRONG]   'behaviourist learners' driven by reward
        -> That's reinforcement-style learning, not the 'parrot' critique.

10. Across a population, IQ scores are distributed…
   1) [CORRECT] normally, with most people near the middle
        -> Right — a bell curve centred near 100.
   2) [WRONG]   uniformly, with every score equally common
        -> A flat distribution doesn't match how IQ scores cluster.
   3) [WRONG]   bimodally, clustering at two extremes
        -> Scores don't pile up at two peaks.
   4) [WRONG]   skewed, with most people scoring high
        -> Most people are near the average, not bunched at the top.

11. Behaviour is generally most resistant to extinction when reinforcement is…
   1) [CORRECT] occasional and unpredictable
        -> Right — intermittent reinforcement makes behaviour most extinction-resistant.
   2) [WRONG]   present on every single trial
        -> Constant reinforcement learns fast but extinguishes fast once it stops.
   3) [WRONG]   given only on the very first trial
        -> A single early reward rarely sustains behaviour.
   4) [WRONG]   tied to a fixed daily clock
        -> A clock schedule isn't what stabilises a learned response here.


## Learning & Memory  (13 questions)
--------------------------------------------------

1. The standard model divides memory into three stages:
   1) [CORRECT] encoding, storage, retrieval
        -> Right — the three classic memory stages.
   2) [WRONG]   input, processing, output
        -> That's a generic computing flow, not the memory model's terms.
   3) [WRONG]   perception, attention, recall
        -> Real processes, but not the three-stage memory model.
   4) [WRONG]   sensing, encoding, forgetting
        -> Forgetting isn't one of the three stages; retrieval is.

2. The classic 'magical number' for short-term memory capacity is about…
   1) [CORRECT] seven items, give or take two
        -> Right — 7±2 (often estimated closer to 4–5 today).
   2) [WRONG]   twenty items, give or take five
        -> Far too high for short-term capacity.
   3) [WRONG]   around fifty items
        -> Nowhere near short-term capacity.
   4) [WRONG]   effectively unlimited
        -> Short-term memory is sharply limited, not unlimited.

3. A memory 'schema' is best described as…
   1) [CORRECT] an organised structure of prior experience that guides recall
        -> Right — we keep the gist and reconstruct details.
   2) [WRONG]   a verbatim recording of a single event
        -> Memory isn't a literal recording; schemas reshape recall.
   3) [WRONG]   a temporary buffer holding a few items
        -> That's working/short-term memory, not a schema.
   4) [WRONG]   an automatic stimulus–response reflex
        -> That's conditioning, not an organised knowledge structure.

4. If a cue already reliably predicts an outcome, the brain often ignores a redundant new cue. This is…
   1) [CORRECT] competition among predictors
        -> Right — a redundant cue is blocked to save effort.
   2) [WRONG]   configural learning
        -> That's binding features into one bundle, not blocking a redundant cue.
   3) [WRONG]   generalization
        -> That's transfer to similar situations, not ignoring redundant cues.
   4) [WRONG]   temporal contiguity
        -> That's association from co-activation in time.

5. Recognising a face as a single 'bundle' rather than learning hair and eyes separately illustrates…
   1) [CORRECT] configural learning
        -> Right — features stored together as a bundle.
   2) [WRONG]   competition among predictors
        -> That's ignoring a redundant cue, not bundling features.
   3) [WRONG]   extinction
        -> That's a learned link fading when reinforcement stops.
   4) [WRONG]   temporal contiguity
        -> That's association from things co-occurring in time.

6. Two events activated close together in time become associated (the principle behind priming and Word2Vec). This is…
   1) [CORRECT] temporal contiguity
        -> Right — co-activated processes get linked.
   2) [WRONG]   competition among predictors
        -> That's blocking a redundant cue.
   3) [WRONG]   generalization
        -> That's transfer to similar situations.
   4) [WRONG]   unlearning
        -> That's weakening an existing association, not forming one.

7. Applying knowledge to a new situation, more easily the more alike the two are, is…
   1) [CORRECT] generalization
        -> Right — transfer is easier the more similar the situations.
   2) [WRONG]   extinction
        -> That's a response fading when its consequence stops.
   3) [WRONG]   configural learning
        -> That's binding features into a bundle.
   4) [WRONG]   encoding
        -> That's getting information into memory in the first place.

8. A cue repeatedly presented without its usual consequence gradually loses its effect. This is…
   1) [CORRECT] extinction
        -> Right — the link fades when the consequence stops appearing.
   2) [WRONG]   configural learning
        -> That's bundling features together.
   3) [WRONG]   generalization
        -> That's transferring a response to similar situations.
   4) [WRONG]   encoding
        -> That's the initial processing of input into memory.

9. The brain 'filling in' the retinal blind spot is used to argue that perception is best described as…
   1) [CORRECT] construction — the brain builds and completes the input
        -> Right — perception organises and fills in the signal.
   2) [WRONG]   transmission — the eye relays an exact image
        -> The eye doesn't pass on a perfect copy; the brain constructs.
   3) [WRONG]   selection — only colour information is kept
        -> Perception isn't just colour filtering.
   4) [WRONG]   reflection — the world is mirrored unchanged
        -> Perception actively shapes input, not mirrors it.

10. Viewers counting passes fail to notice a person in a gorilla suit walk through. This is…
   1) [CORRECT] inattentional blindness
        -> Right — an obvious event missed because attention is elsewhere.
   2) [WRONG]   the retinal blind spot
        -> That's a physiological gap the brain fills, not an attention failure.
   3) [WRONG]   the Stroop effect
        -> That's interference between word meaning and ink colour.
   4) [WRONG]   the magic number
        -> That's short-term memory capacity (7±2).

11. Looking facts up online tends to make people…
   1) [CORRECT] overrate their own knowledge and memory
        -> Right — people mistake the internet's knowledge for their own.
   2) [WRONG]   accurately gauge what they truly know
        -> Access to search actually distorts self-assessment.
   3) [WRONG]   underrate their own abilities
        -> The bias runs the other way — overconfidence.
   4) [WRONG]   forget how to search effectively
        -> Searching doesn't degrade search skill here; it inflates self-knowledge.

12. In the essay-writing study, heavier reliance on an LLM was linked with…
   1) [CORRECT] lower ownership and weaker memory of the text
        -> Right — less load, but less ownership, quality and recall ('cognitive debt').
   2) [WRONG]   higher ownership and stronger recall
        -> It's the reverse — reliance lowered ownership and recall.
   3) [WRONG]   better grades with greater effort
        -> Reliance reduced effort, and didn't simply raise grades.
   4) [WRONG]   no change on any measure
        -> There were clear differences in ownership and memory.

13. Information processed 'deeply' by meaning, rather than 'superficially' by surface form, is later…
   1) [CORRECT] recalled better
        -> Right — meaning-based processing aids retrieval.
   2) [WRONG]   recalled worse
        -> Deep processing helps, not hurts, recall.
   3) [WRONG]   recalled only with strong cues
        -> Deep encoding makes recall easier, not cue-dependent.
   4) [WRONG]   recalled identically
        -> Depth of processing does change later recall.

--------------------------------------------------

1. 'Creative destruction' describes how innovation…
   1) [CORRECT] upsets the existing economic equilibrium
        -> Right — innovation disrupts the status quo.
   2) [WRONG]   locks in the existing equilibrium
        -> The opposite — it breaks, not freezes, the equilibrium.
   3) [WRONG]   leaves the equilibrium untouched
        -> Innovation changes the equilibrium by definition here.
   4) [WRONG]   restores a previously lost equilibrium
        -> It creates new disruption, not restoration.

2. Which is one of the 'three big No's' of social innovation?
   1) [CORRECT] causing negative social outcomes
        -> Right — one of the three 'No's'.
   2) [WRONG]   generating a financial profit
        -> Profit isn't one of the 'No's'.
   3) [WRONG]   scaling to many users
        -> Scaling isn't a 'No'.
   4) [WRONG]   relying on new technology
        -> Using tech isn't itself a 'No'.

3. A frequent reason a social innovation has 'no impact' is that…
   1) [CORRECT] the real user and use-case were never studied
        -> Right — failing to study the user is a common cause.
   2) [WRONG]   it was launched too cautiously
        -> Caution isn't the cited reason for no impact.
   3) [WRONG]   it relied too little on technology
        -> Low-tech isn't the issue; misunderstanding users is.
   4) [WRONG]   it was evaluated too thoroughly
        -> Over-evaluation isn't the failure mode described.

4. 'Diffusion of innovations' refers to how an innovation…
   1) [CORRECT] spreads through a social system over time
        -> Right — communication of an innovation over time.
   2) [WRONG]   is invented and prototyped
        -> That's the creation phase, not diffusion.
   3) [WRONG]   is patented and licensed
        -> That's legal/commercial protection, not spread.
   4) [WRONG]   is withdrawn from a market
        -> That's the opposite of diffusion.
